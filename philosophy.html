Philosophy

Why we exist.

The companies that win are rarely the ones with the best product. They're the ones that understand their customers more deeply, learn faster, and make commercial decisions with more confidence. Most organizations already have the information they need to compete this way. Very few have built the system to act on it. We kept seeing that gap, company after company, and built Signal to close it.

The problem we solve

The same three problems, company after company.

Across two decades of commercial work with more than fifty B2B companies, we keep seeing the same three problems. Each ties directly to revenue growth, cost to serve, customer retention, or competitive defensibility.

01
B2B customer strategy is built on assumptions.
Most organizations set customer strategy based on last year's performance, internal instinct, and guesses about what customers want next. Interviews sit in folders. Win-loss notes live in one person's head. Churn is discovered at renewal, months after the decision was already made. The data exists. It just never becomes a decision.
Teams waste time, budget, and sales effort executing against a strategy built on guesses rather than evidence.
02
Customer intelligence is constantly created, but almost never synthesized into a decision.
Every customer conversation, service interaction, and renewal discussion produces signal. Across sales, marketing, product, and customer success, the organization is constantly learning what customers want, what frustrates them, and what would make them leave. Almost none of it reaches the people making strategic decisions in a form they can act on.
The gap between what customers are telling the organization and what leadership is deciding is where commercial performance is lost.
03
Most companies don't actually know who their best customers are.
Not all customers create equal value. Some are high-revenue but expensive to serve. Some are smaller but loyal, profitable, and likely to expand. Without a systematic way to connect customer intelligence to commercial performance, organizations end up investing in the wrong relationships or failing to protect the right ones.
Without clarity on which customers actually drive value, organizations protect the wrong relationships and neglect the right ones.
What we believe

Five principles behind everything we build.

Principle 01
Intelligence only matters if it changes a decision.
A report that sits in a deck isn't intelligence, no matter how thorough it is. We design every output to end in a decision, not an observation.
Principle 02
Commercial decisions should compound.
Most companies start over every time they run a new study. We built Signal so each decision builds on the last one, instead of starting from zero.
Principle 03
Your best customers teach you how to find the next ones.
Intuition usually builds the ideal customer profile. We believe evidence should build it instead, from the patterns your best customers already leave behind.
Principle 04
Not all churn is worth preventing.
Retention programs that treat every loss as equally bad end up protecting the wrong accounts. Some customers cost more to keep than they're worth. The real skill is knowing which relationships deserve the fight, and moving early enough for it to matter.
Principle 05
Customer knowledge should belong to the organization, not one employee.
An account manager's judgment usually leaves the building with them. We treat that judgment as an asset the business owns, not a habit that resets with every hire.
Customer intelligence is infrastructure

Better decisions should compound, not restart.

We built Signal to run on a loop, not a project timeline. Every customer conversation becomes structured intelligence. Every piece of intelligence informs a commercial decision. Every decision produces an outcome that sharpens the next round of understanding. That loop is what lets advantage compound instead of resetting every quarter.

2

3

4

5

Built by practitioners

Not created in a research lab.

We started Signal after two decades of helping commercial organizations answer the same questions, solve the same problems, and repeat the same work by hand. The platform captures that experience in software, not to replace judgment, but to give good judgment better information.

That experience surfaced a consistent pattern: the companies that win commercially are not always the ones with the best product. They are the ones with the clearest picture of who they're serving and why those customers chose them in the first place.
